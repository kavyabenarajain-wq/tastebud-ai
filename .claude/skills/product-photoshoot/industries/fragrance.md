# Fragrance — Product Photoshoot Playbook

Loaded by [product-photoshoot/SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) when the uploaded product is perfume/cologne/mist. The engine owns the realism bar, brand-lock, six-angle system, and QC. This file owns the **taste**: how fragrance specifically has to be shot.

> The third Beauty sub-type, alongside [skincare](beauty-skincare.md) and [makeup](makeup.md) — and the most distinct. Skincare sells a result; makeup sells colour; **fragrance sells something invisible.**

---

## What fragrance is really selling — the core problem

You cannot photograph a smell. The bottle is opaque to its own benefit. So every strong fragrance image solves this one of two ways — and the best do both:

1. **Make the scent visible through its notes** — surround the bottle with what it smells like (cherries, coconut, lavender, vanilla pods, figs, citrus, florals, spices). The viewer reads the scent pyramid off the props.
2. **Evoke the feeling** — through dramatic light, atmosphere (smoke, mist, flame), and skin/sensuality. Mood stands in for scent.

This is why fragrance is **moodier, more cinematic, and more sensual** than skincare or makeup. The bottle is also an iconic design object — reproduce its exact silhouette, faceting, cap, and atomizer; fragrance buyers recognise the bottle.

---

## The shot archetypes

Pick the archetype per shot. A strong fragrance set mixes 3–4; it never returns six packshots.

### 1. Scent-note explosion / ingredient world — *the signature fragrance move*
The scent told literally: cherries spilling around the bottle, a cracked coconut, pineapple slices, a pile of figs and grapes, lavender + coconut, vanilla pods in a martini glass, citrus, florals. The dramatic version: spices and botanicals flying out of a splash (cinnamon, anise, fruit) for warm/oriental scents.
- **Recipe:** props are the literal top/heart/base notes; they echo the juice colour; keep produce fresh and glossy; the bottle stays hero amid the world.
- **Proves:** what it smells like, in one frame.

### 2. Dramatic chiaroscuro / spotlight hero — *fragrance's luxury default*
Bottle on a dark or moody ground, a single dramatic key or a coloured gradient glow behind it (sunset orange, purple, deep red), a clean reflection on a glossy surface.
- **Recipe:** deep shadows, one sculptural light, coloured gel or gradient backdrop keyed to the scent family; controlled refraction through the glass; mirror-glossy base.
- **Proves:** mystery, premium, desire.

### 3. Held to skin / the gesture of wearing
Hand presenting the bottle on an open palm, held to the neck or décolleté, sprayed at the wrist or pulse point, brought near the face.
- **Recipe:** warm intimate light, real skin, believable grip; for the spray, catch the atomizer mid-gesture.
- **Proves:** intimacy and how it's worn — fragrance is body-adjacent.

### 4. Spray / mist in action
The spray caught mid-air as a fine plume, often backlit so the mist glows.
- **Recipe:** backlight or rim light to reveal the mist; dark or simple ground for contrast; crisp atomizer; the plume reads as fine droplets, not fog.
- **Proves:** the product in use, the atomization moment.

### 5. Lifestyle — pocket, fabric, seduction
Bottle tucked in a denim pocket, resting on a leather jacket, on flowing silk/satin, on rumpled bedsheets beside a vintage phone, on a straw hat at the beach.
- **Recipe:** styled but believable; soft daylight or warm directional sun; props chosen for the brand's persona (seductive, clean, playful, old-money).
- **Proves:** the life and mood the scent belongs to.

### 6. Plinth / podium romantic-minimal
Bottle on travertine/stone/plaster, dressed with rose or flower petals, dried botanicals, soft shadow.
- **Recipe:** like the skincare podium but more romantic — petals, florals, warm soft light, heavy negative space.
- **Proves:** elegance and craft.

### 7. Model + bottle editorial (sensual / muse)
Model holding or presenting the bottle near the face, sunlit skin, sensual styling — the fragrance "muse" (often celebrity-fronted).
- **Recipe:** beauty-editorial light, glowing skin, the bottle integrated into a gesture; the bottle label and juice stay true.
- **Proves:** aspiration and the person who wears it.

### 8. Atmosphere — smoke, flame, shadow play
Candle flame beside the bottle, smoke or fog rolling across a moody set, a cast shadow of a figure reaching for the bottle, hand or palm-frond shadows, playing cards + pearls still life.
- **Recipe:** cinematic, low-key; real flame glow or rolling haze; physically consistent shadows.
- **Proves:** mystique — pure mood standing in for scent.

## Craft constants (how fragrance differs from skincare & makeup)

- **Light is dramatic, not clean.** Chiaroscuro, coloured gradient glows, backlight, spotlight, deeper shadows. Where skincare wants soft trustworthy light, fragrance wants cinematic and luxurious. (Body mists and fresh/clean brands are the exception — they can go bright and airy.)
- **Palette is keyed to the scent family**, not to neutrals (skincare) or the shade (makeup):
  - **Fresh / aquatic / citrus** → blue, green, water, glass, bright daylight.
  - **Floral** → pink, cream, soft romantic light, petals.
  - **Gourmand / sweet** → amber, caramel, warm gold, food notes.
  - **Woody / oriental / spicy** → dark, deep red, gold, smoke, jewel tones.
- **The bottle is a sculptural hero.** Faceted glass, the juice colour, the cap (jewel, ball, magnet, weighted), the atomizer — all iconic and must be reproduced exactly. Refraction and caustics carry the luxury.
- **Sensuality and intimacy** — skin, silk, the spray gesture, pulse points. More body-adjacent than skincare, more grown-up than makeup.
- **Mood over information.** Fragrance tolerates (wants) atmosphere, negative space, and ambiguity that skincare's clarity-first imagery avoids.

## Substance & material specifics (extends the engine's substance rules)

- **The juice:** true colour and fill level, refraction through faceted glass, caustics where light passes through onto the surface. Coloured juice (amber, pink, green, blue) is part of the brand ID — match it.
- **The glass bottle:** exact silhouette and faceting; controlled highlights on the shoulders and edges, not blown-out blobs; frosted glass diffuses to a soft glow; heavy glass reads weighty.
- **The cap & atomizer:** reproduce exactly — jewel cap, weighted ball, magnetic cap, classic atomizer/sprayer. A wrong cap is an instant reject.
- **The mist:** a fine, believable spray plume with backlight, never a vague fog.
- **Note props:** fresh, glossy, just-placed produce and botanicals; flames real and warm; smoke/haze physically plausible.

The brand's name, logo, and bottle engraving stay sharp and legible — warped perfume-house text is an instant reject.

## Default move (safe, on-brand fragrance recipe)

When the brief is thin: **read the scent family → key the palette and light to it** → choose **dramatic chiaroscuro hero** or **scent-note world** as the lead → nail the **glass refraction, juice colour, and exact cap** → add intimacy (a hand, skin, silk) or atmosphere (petals, soft smoke) → keep generous, confident negative space. Pull exact palette, surface, and mood from the Brand Profile first; if the scent family is unknown, infer it from the juice colour and bottle and ask only if it's load-bearing.

## Six-angle adaptation

The engine's six angles still apply; bias them for fragrance:
- **Low hero angle** makes the bottle monumental — strong for luxury fragrance.
- **Three-quarter** best shows faceting and the cap.
- **Macro** carries refraction, the engraving, and a single light edge on the glass.
- Swap in a **scent-note world** frame and a **held-to-skin or spray** frame over a plain side profile.
- Keep the light logic and scent-family palette consistent so the set reads as one campaign.

---

## Category do-not list (on top of the Brand Profile's)

- No wrong cap, atomizer, or bottle silhouette — the bottle is iconic, reproduce it exactly.
- No flat, even lighting that kills the drama (unless it's a fresh/clean brand that wants bright).
- No blown-out highlight blobs on the glass; refraction and caustics must read true.
- No vague fog standing in for a spray — mist must be fine droplets.
- No scent-note props that contradict the actual scent family.
- No warped perfume-house name or engraving.
- No plastic/CGI glass or skin.
