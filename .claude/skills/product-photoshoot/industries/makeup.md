# Makeup — Product Photoshoot Playbook

Loaded by [product-photoshoot/SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) when the uploaded product is colour cosmetics. The engine still owns the realism bar, brand-lock, six-angle system, and QC. This file owns the **taste**: how makeup specifically has to be shot to read like the category's best work.

---

## What makeup is really selling

Skincare sells a *result you can't see yet* (clarity, hydration) so it leans clean, clinical, calm. **Makeup sells the colour and the finish — right now, on the body.** The shade *is* the product. So the two things this category must always nail:

1. **Colour payoff** — the exact shade, rich and true, never muddy or washed out.
2. **Finish / texture** — matte, satin, cream, glaze, metallic, shimmer. The viewer must be able to *feel* whether it's a dewy gloss or a velvet matte.

If a makeup shot doesn't prove the colour and the finish, it has failed — no matter how pretty the staging.

---

## The shot archetypes

Pick the archetype per shot. A strong makeup set mixes 3–4 of these; it never returns six packshots.

### 1. Swatch / texture as hero — *the signature makeup move*
The pigment itself, smeared, dragged, or dolloped — often gridded and labelled by shade ("sugar glaze", "peach glaze", "cinnamon sugar"). This is the most makeup-specific shot there is and almost every set should include one.
- **Recipe:** neutral cream/paper or matched-tone surface; soft raking light to carve the texture; swatches with real spread, a soft tapered edge, and true viscosity (cream peaks, gloss glistens, powder sits fine and matte). Show the product beside or partly in its own swatch.
- **Proves:** colour + finish in one frame.

### 2. Application in action / model-as-canvas
Lipstick going on, blush brush on the cheek, mascara mid-stroke, a compact held to the eye. The face shows the *result* — glossed lip, flushed cheek, lit skin.
- **Recipe:** clean beauty light (soft, frontal-ish, flattering), real skin with visible pores and natural texture (never plastic), the product held in a believable grip. The applied colour on the face must match the product's shade exactly.
- **Proves:** how the colour actually wears.

### 3. Hand-held shade fan
One or several hands holding the range — ideally across different skin tones — fanned or crossed.
- **Recipe:** simple seamless or matched backdrop; even, glossy beauty light; neutral or considered nails; bottles/tubes turned so labels stay legible.
- **Proves:** shade range and inclusivity in a single hero.

### 4. Saturated colour-block studio
Background keyed *to the shade* — pink-on-pink, red-on-red, a complementary gradient. Bold, confident, graphic. The opposite of skincare's neutral sand.
- **Recipe:** seamless saturated paper or gradient; often a glossy reflective tabletop with a clean mirror reflection; one crisp directional highlight on the cap/bullet. Colour gels welcome.
- **Proves:** brand confidence + makes the shade unmissable.

### 5. Edible / indulgent pairing — *desire, not ingredients*
Makeup pairs with **craving**, not actives: cherries in a martini glass, coffee beans spilling from a cup, berries on a swirl of cream, ice-cream cones, gloss styled as soft-serve, fruit that echoes the shade.
- **Recipe:** the food echoes the product colour (a berry-red tint with raspberries; a "latte" nude with coffee); keep food fresh and glossy, never set too long; product stays the hero, food supports.
- **Proves:** the shade's mood and "flavour".

### 6. Lifestyle flat lay with personality props
The product inside a life: denim back-pocket, a designer bag spill, gingham picnic, kiss-print backdrop, a desk. "My must-haves," not a lab.
- **Recipe:** styled but believable clutter; soft daylight or warm directional sun with crisp shadows; props chosen for the brand's persona (luxe vs. playful vs. clean-girl).
- **Proves:** who this is for and where it lives.

### 7. Mirror & reflective reveal
Compact mirror held up reflecting an eye or swatches; a gold dome lifting off a tray; glitter beds; glossy reflective sweeps.
- **Recipe:** controlled reflections (real, physically consistent — no impossible mirror logic); glamour-forward light; metallic/glass surfaces.
- **Proves:** glamour and the reflective/shimmer finishes.

### 8. Floating / sculptural stack
Tubes suspended in a cloud sky; stacked balm "pebbles"; clustered blush sticks. Playful, modern, graphic — best for younger colour-brands.
- **Recipe:** clean gradient or sky; consistent soft shadowing so floats still feel grounded in light logic; arrangement reads as designed, not accidental.
- **Proves:** energy and the product as object.

## Craft constants (how makeup differs from skincare)

- **Colour is the protagonist.** Backgrounds are chosen to *flatter or match* the shade, not to recede. Skincare = neutral and quiet; makeup = chromatic and brand-keyed. Pull the backdrop from the product's own colour or its complement unless the Brand Profile says otherwise.
- **Finish/texture proof is mandatory.** Every set needs at least one swatch or glossy macro that reads the finish: matte = velvet, no specular; satin = soft sheen; cream = buttery edge; gloss/glaze = wet, high specular, reflective; metallic/shimmer = directional sparkle that travels with the light.
- **Higher energy and indulgence** than skincare — expressive models, food cravings, glossy wet finishes, glitter, playful props.
- **Reflective surfaces and mirrors** do the staging work that stone and linen do in skincare.
- **Light:** clean beauty light for faces; for product, warm-directional with crisp shadows *or* saturated colour-gel studio. Avoid flat, even, shadowless lighting — it kills the finish read.

## Substance & material specifics (extends the engine's substance rules)

- **Lipstick / bullet:** show the true bullet shape and the finish in the body — cream catches a soft highlight, matte stays velvet, satin has a gentle sheen. A swatch drag nearby sells the payoff. Never blunt or melt the bullet tip unless intentionally styled.
- **Gloss / lip oil:** must read **wet and reflective** — high specular hits, a glassy meniscus, the doe-foot or applicator with a clinging drop. The "glazed" look is the whole point.
- **Liquid foundation / tint:** show a believable drop or drag swatch in the exact shade; check the undertone is true. Often paired with a skin swatch to prove the match.
- **Powder / pressed (blush, shadow, setting):** fine grain, a pressed or lightly-kicked-up loose surface, a brush loaded with pigment. No plastic-flat powder.
- **Cream blush / stick:** buttery, slightly dewy swatch with a soft melt at the edge.
- **Mascara / liner:** clean wand with product on the bristles (not a dry wand); crisp tip on a liner; a graphic stroke swatch sells the formula.
- **Palettes:** every pan colour true and distinct; pans clean; if open, a believable angle that shows the shade story. Mirror reflection physically correct.
- **Glitter / shimmer:** sparkle is directional and travels with the light — never a static dotted texture map.

In all cases the brand's text, shade name, and finish stay sharp and legible — warped shade names are an instant reject.

## Default move (safe, on-brand makeup recipe)

When the brief is thin: **lead with the shade** (backdrop matched or complementary to the product colour) → **prove the texture** with at least one swatch or glossy macro → **show it on a face or in a hand** → optionally add one indulgent food/lifestyle prop that echoes the shade → keep the finish (matte / cream / glaze / metallic) physically true. Pull exact palette, surface, and mood from the Brand Profile first.

## Six-angle adaptation

The engine's six angles still apply; bias them for makeup:
- **Macro close-up** is non-negotiable here — it carries the finish and swatch.
- **Three-quarter** flatters bullets, tubes, and compacts best.
- Add or swap in a **model application** frame and a **swatch/texture** frame; these often beat a plain side profile for makeup.
- Keep the shade, surface, and finish consistent so the set reads as one campaign.

---

## Category do-not list (on top of the Brand Profile's)

- No muddy, washed-out, or shifted shade — colour must match the product exactly.
- No flat, shadowless lighting that flattens the finish.
- No plastic/CGI skin on models — real pores, real texture, natural asymmetry.
- No dry mascara wands or empty applicators.
- No warped shade names or label text.
- No impossible mirror/reflection logic.
- No swatch that ignores viscosity (gloss that looks matte, powder that looks wet).
