# Fashion & Apparel — Product Photoshoot Playbook

Loaded by [product-photoshoot/SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) when the uploaded product is a garment. The engine owns the realism bar, brand-lock, six-angle system, and QC. This file owns the **taste**: how clothing specifically has to be shot to read like the category's best work.

---

## The first decision: on-model or off-model

Apparel is the one category that is routinely shot **two completely different ways**, and you must pick before anything else:

- **Off-model — the garment as product.** Flat lay, ghost-mannequin (invisible body), hung on a hanger/rack/clothesline, draped over a chair/stool, folded stack, or styled still life. **This is what the present skill owns.** The fabric, fit, drape, colour, and graphic *are* the subject; no human in frame.
- **On-model — the garment worn.** Studio, street, editorial, lifestyle, group. The moment a human body wears the piece, **hand off to the `model-photoshoot` skill** — it owns the human-realism bar (skin, hands, hair, identity persistence) and the on-model coverage system. Use this apparel file alongside it for the *garment* truth (fabric, fit, print legibility), but model-photoshoot drives.

**Rule of thumb:** if the brief or the brand's reference shows a person wearing it → model-photoshoot leads. If it shows the garment alone → stay here. When ambiguous, a strong apparel set often does **both**: a hero on-model look plus off-model flat-lay/detail support shots. Confirm the split with the client before generating.

> The references behind this playbook split almost exactly down the "with model" / "without model" line — Pinterest even filters them that way. Respect that; they are different crafts.

## What apparel is really selling

Skincare sells a result; F&B sells appetite. **Apparel sells fit, fabric, and identity** — *how it hangs, how it feels, and who you become wearing it.* Three things every apparel shot must serve:

1. **The cut & fit** — silhouette, length, how it falls on a body or a form. Oversized reads oversized; tailored reads sharp.
2. **The fabric & texture** — the viewer must *feel* the hand of it: the slub of linen, the loft of a knit, the wash and weight of denim, the drape of silk, the brush of fleece.
3. **The identity** — minimal-premium, streetwear, vintage, playful, athletic. The styling, surface, and light carry the persona as much as the garment does.

If a shot doesn't prove fit, fabric, **and** the brand's identity, it has failed — however nice the lighting.

## Brand-worlds (read the brand, commit to one)

Apparel spans wildly different aesthetics. Lock the world first:

- **Minimal / premium** → neutral seamless (sand, bone, grey), quiet directional light, soft long shadow; elevated still life or a single clean look; lots of negative space; REISS / The Row / quiet-luxury knitwear energy.
- **Streetwear** → urban texture, concrete, motion blur, harsh flash, graphic backs to camera, racks and piles; Gallery Dept / Fear of God / Stüssy energy; the graphic and the drop *are* the point.
- **Playful / D2C** → sunny saturated colour, clotheslines and props, lifestyle joy, hands and movement; young and social.
- **Vintage / thrift / archival** → market stalls, packed racks, folded jersey stacks, film grain, lived-in styling; provenance and the hunt.

## Off-model archetypes (this skill's core)

Pick per shot. A strong off-model set mixes 3–4; it never returns six identical hanger shots.

### 1. Flat lay — *the workhorse off-model move*
The garment laid flat, styled from above, often with accessories (sunglasses, cap, jewellery, shoes, a folded pair of jeans) that build the look and the persona.
- **Recipe:** clean surface keyed to the brand-world; even soft top light or a gentle rake to show texture; garment arranged with intentional folds (a sleeve bent, a natural drape), not ironed-corpse-flat; props echo the identity.
- **Proves:** the full look, the styling story, the print/graphic — all at once.

### 2. Ghost mannequin / invisible body
The garment holding a 3D worn shape with no visible model or form — the e-commerce standard for showing true fit and structure.
- **Recipe:** clean seamless; the garment filled to a believable body volume; collar, cuffs, hem sitting as if worn; no mannequin edges, no neck hole gaping wrong.
- **Proves:** real fit and silhouette without a person.

### 3. Hung — hanger, rack, or clothesline
On a single hanger against a wall, on a rolling rack (full collection / lookbook), or pegged to a clothesline against sky.
- **Recipe:** hanger shot is clean and centred with natural shoulder fall; rack shot shows the range with one hero forward; clothesline is sunny, breezy, playful (garment lifting in wind). Wood, chrome, or wire hangers chosen to match the world.
- **Proves:** drape, the collection, or breezy lifestyle mood.

### 4. Draped / propped still life
Garment thrown over a chair, stool, ladder, or bench; a blazer on a chrome chair with bag and heels beside it; a knit folded on a wooden stool outdoors.
- **Recipe:** one considered surface/prop; warm directional light and a real cast shadow; the drape natural and gravity-true; supporting props build the outfit.
- **Proves:** the hand of the fabric and the lifestyle context.

### 5. Folded stack
Tees or denim folded and stacked, sometimes a tall colour-graded tower; the merchandised, tactile, "feel the cotton" shot.
- **Recipe:** crisp clean folds, true fabric colour per layer, soft light to read the weave; a brand label or woven tab turned forward.
- **Proves:** range, colourway, fabric quality, retail abundance.

### 6. Styled still life with accessories — *the editorial off-model flex*
The garment as the centre of a deliberately composed tableau — outerwear with bag, shoes, sunglasses, and jewellery arranged like a flat lay stood up, often on warm seamless with long raking light.
- **Recipe:** premium directional light, sculpted shadow, a tight curated edit of objects; everything in one colour story.
- **Proves:** the whole look and the brand's taste level.

### 7. Detail / fabric macro
Close on the weave, the rib of a knit, the wash and selvedge of denim, the stitch, the embroidery, the screenprint, the woven label, the hardware (zip, button, drawcord).
- **Recipe:** raking light to carve texture; shallow depth; the print/graphic sharp and legible.
- **Proves:** fabric quality and construction — where premium is won.

### 8. Graphic-back / print hero
The garment pinned or hung flat to show the back print or front graphic full and undistorted (huge in streetwear and merch).
- **Recipe:** garment flattened against a wall or surface so the graphic reads at true proportion; even light; colours true.
- **Proves:** the artwork and the drop — often the whole reason the piece exists.

## On-model archetypes (hand to model-photoshoot — listed so you brief it right)

When the shoot goes on-model, **model-photoshoot leads**; bias it toward these apparel-native looks:

1. **Studio look on seamless** — clean full-length, true colour, the fit head-to-toe.
2. **Street / outdoor lifestyle** — urban or sunlit location, candid walking energy, the garment in the wild.
3. **Editorial movement** — motion blur, a jump, fabric caught mid-air; mood over catalogue.
4. **Group / cast** — several models, the range as a community, denim-on-steps energy.
5. **Back-to-camera / graphic** — showing the back print, especially streetwear.
6. **Detail-on-body** — cropped to show fit at the waist, cuff, or how a fabric falls on a real leg.

Always: the *garment* truth from this file (true fabric, fit, legible print) plus the *human* truth from model-photoshoot (real skin, hands, identity).

## Substance & material rules (extends the engine's substance rules)

Fabric **is** the substance here — render its physics:

- **Drape & gravity.** Cloth hangs and folds by weight: silk and rayon flow and pool; heavy wool holds structure; jersey clings soft; linen creases crisp; denim breaks stiff. Folds must obey gravity from real support points — no floating or impossible drape.
- **Weave & knit.** Show the actual construction: ribbed and cabled knits with loft and stitch definition, the diagonal twill of denim, the slub of linen, the smooth face of poplin, the pile of fleece/terry, the sheen of satin.
- **Wash & finish.** Denim wash, fade, whiskering, and selvedge read true; garment-dye and acid-wash character preserved; a crisp white stays clean, a vintage tee reads soft and lived-in.
- **Fit & volume.** Oversized looks oversized, tailored looks sharp; ghost-mannequin and worn shots hold believable body volume; shoulders, collars, cuffs, and hems sit correctly.
- **Print, graphic & text — legibility is law.** Screenprints, logos, slogan text, and embroidery must be sharp, correctly spelled, and at true proportion on the garment's curve. **Warped or invented graphic text is the single fastest apparel tell and an automatic reject.**
- **Colour truth.** The garment's exact colour and colourway, unshifted by the background or grade. Black reads as the right black (warm/cool); a specific wash or pastel stays itself.
- **Construction detail.** Stitching, seams, hardware (zips, buttons, rivets, drawcords), woven labels, and trims render real and consistent — no melted buttons or duplicated zips.

## Light & surface

- **Minimal / premium** → soft directional key, one long sculpted shadow, neutral seamless or warm sand; calm and spacious.
- **Streetwear** → harder light or direct flash, concrete / brick / metal / transit surfaces, deliberate grain and motion.
- **Playful / D2C** → bright sunny daylight, saturated or pastel grounds, sky, grass, clotheslines.
- **Vintage / thrift** → ambient natural light, film grain, markets, packed racks, worn wood.
- Across any world: keep **fabric texture readable** — flat shadowless light kills the hand of the cloth, which is half the sell.

## Default move (safe, on-brand apparel recipe)

When the brief is thin: **decide on-model vs off-model first (confirm with the client) → read the brand into one of the four worlds → if off-model, lead with a styled flat lay or a hung/draped hero, add a fabric/print macro, add a folded stack or accessory still life for the look → if on-model, hand to model-photoshoot and brief a clean studio full-length plus a lifestyle/movement frame.** Protect drape, fit, colour, and print legibility above all. Pull exact palette, surface, styling, and persona from the Brand Profile first.

## Six-angle adaptation

The engine's six angles still apply; bias them for apparel:
- **Front and back are both mandatory** — the back carries graphics, fit, and construction; never skip it.
- **Flat-lay overhead** replaces the plain top-down for off-model.
- **Macro** is essential — it carries fabric, weave, and print.
- Swap a plain side profile for a **drape/hung** frame that shows how the cloth falls.
- For on-model, defer to model-photoshoot's coverage set.
- Keep brand-world, light, surface, and styling consistent so the set reads as one collection, not scattered pieces.

## Category do-not list (on top of the Brand Profile's)

- No warped, misspelled, or invented graphic/slogan/logo text; no print distorted off the garment's true proportion.
- No impossible drape or floating folds — cloth obeys gravity from real support points.
- No wrong fabric behaviour — stiff silk, limp denim, a knit with no loft, a flat dead surface where texture is the sell.
- No colour shifted off the true garment colourway by the background or grade.
- No visible mannequin edges, gaping neck, or broken body volume in ghost-mannequin/worn shots.
- No melted or duplicated hardware (buttons, zips, rivets), seams, or labels.
- No flat shadowless lighting that erases fabric texture (unless a deliberately flat e-com look is briefed).
- No human in frame when an off-model shot was requested — and conversely, route on-model work to the model-photoshoot skill rather than faking a body here.
